The Mother Wit Conference

The Mother Wit Conference started with a conversation between a mother & a daughter, and evolved into a dialogue between women of all ages and walks of life. Now it is a forum for men, women, families and communities to share, receive, transform and HEAL ♥

The Ashmun Institute was established April 29th 1854. The Ashmun Institute was the first institute of higher learning created to educate African-Americans, renamed Lincoln University in 1866.

Dr. Rebecca Lee Crumpler becomes the first African-American woman in the US to earn an MD in 1864

160 Years Ago

On April 8, 1864, the Thirteenth Amendment abolishing slavary in the US was passed by Senate

160 Years Ago

Maggie Lena Walker, born on July 15, 1864, was an African American entrepreneur, civic leader, and teacher who became the first Black woman to establish and become president of a bank in the United States in 1903.

120 Years Ago

New York City's Subway began operating on October 27th, 1904

100 Years Ago

The Harlem Renaissance began in 1924. This marked the beginning of an intellectual and cultural revival of African-American music, dance, art, fashion, literature, theater, politics and scholarship centered in Harlem, spanning the 1920s and 1930s

100 Years Ago

Shirley Anita Chisholm was born. Chisholm was an American politician who, in 1968, became the first black woman to be elected to the United States Congress.

90 Years Ago

Audre Lorde was born. Lorde was an American writer, professor, philosopher, intersectional feminist, poet and civil rights activist. She was a self-described "Black, lesbian, feminist, socialist, mother, warrior, poet" who dedicated her life and talents to confronting different forms of injustice.

90 Years Ago

Zora Neale Hurston published her first novel, Jonah's Gourd Vine, a semi-autobiographical account of her parents' lives. This is the first of over 50 works written by Hurston, including Their Eyes Were Watching God (1937)

80 Years Ago

At age 14, African-American teenage boy George Stinney Jr. becomes the youngest person ever executed by electric chair in the United States after being wrongfully convicted of murder.

80 Years Ago

Akasha Gloria Hull (born December 6, 1944) an American poet, educator, writer, critic, and a member of the Combahee River Collective. Her work in African-American literature and as a Black feminist activist has helped shape Women's Studies

80 Years Ago

Angela Davis, a scholar, author, activist, and black feminist philosopher, was born on January 26, 1944. She is one of the most recognized political activists of the 1960s and 1970s and an advocate for prisoners' rights

80 Years Ago

Alice Walker (born February 9, 1944) is an American novelist, short story writer, poet, and social activist. A prolific author, she became the first African-American woman to win the Pulitzer Prize for Fiction, for her novel The Color Purple.

70 Years Ago

On May 17, 1954, the Supreme Court decided Brown v. Board of Education, arguing that "separate but equal" schools are inherently unequal. The decision legally prohibits school segregation, declaring it unconstitutional.

60 Years Ago

June 14th, 1964, marked the beginning of Freedom Summer, a volunteer Civil Rights project intended to promote voter registration for as many African Americans as possible in Mississippi

60 Years Ago

60 Years Ago

Tensions between African American residents and police in Philadelphia come to a head, leading the Philadelphia Race Riot (August 28-30, 1964), which resulted in 341 injuries and 774 arrests.

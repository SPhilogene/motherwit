10 Years Ago (2014)

Gail Taylor, a Black female farmer in D.C. spearheads the three-year “I Want DC to Grow” campaign that led to the D.C. Urban Farming and Food Security Act of 2014, a bill that incentivized the use of privately-owned land for farming and community gardens.

The Mother Wit Conference

The Mother Wit Conference started with a conversation between a mother & a daughter, and evolved into a dialogue between women of all ages and walks of life. Now it is a forum for men, women, families and communities to share, receive, transform and HEAL ♥

240 Years Ago (1784)

On December 5, 1784, at the age of 31, Phillis Wheatley dies in childbirth and so does her newborn daughter. Wheatly was an enslaved poet who became a literary celebrity in Boston. She was the first Black woman to become a notable poet in the United States.

220 Years Ago (1804)

Haiti gains its independence on January 1, 1804. Victoria Montou, a skilled warrior, midwife, and healer who served as a general of the revolutionary army was honored with the title of Imperial Duchess of the new nation.

170 Years Ago (1854)

On April 29, 1854, Ashmun Institute is established as the first college founded solely for Black American students. Renamed Lincoln University in 1866, it remains the nation's first degree-granting Historically Black College and University (HBCU).

160 Years Ago (1864)

Dr. Rebecca Lee Crumpler, a physician, nurse, and author, becomes the first Black woman in the US to earn an MD in 1864. Crumpler, an advocate of women and children’s health, championed the cause of medical training for nurses, and published a book on preventive health care.

160 Years Ago (1864)

Harriet Jacobs, abolitionist and author of Incidents In the Life of A Slave Girl (1861), opened the Jacobs School with her daughter on January 11, 1864. The school was open to formerly enslaved children and completely controlled by the black community.

160 Years Ago (1864)

Maggie Lena Walker, born on July 15, 1864, was an African American entrepreneur, civic leader, and teacher who became the first Black woman to establish and become president of a bank in the United States in 1903.

110 Years Ago (1914)

Marie Louise Bottineau Baldwin is the first Indigenous law student to graduate from the Washington College of Law in 1914. She served as a Métis Turtle Mountain Band of Chippewa Indians attorney, activist, and suffragist.

40 Years Ago (1984)

Florence Griffith Joyner wins a silver medal in the 200-meter race at the 1984 Olympics in Los Angeles. Four years later (1988) she earned and continues to hold the world record as the fastest woman in the world.

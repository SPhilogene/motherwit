10 Years Ago - 2014

Gail Taylor, a Black female farmer in D.C. spearheads the three-year “I Want DC to Grow” campaign that led to the D.C. Urban Farming and Food Security Act of 2014, a bill that incentivized the use of privately-owned land for farming and community gardens.

The Mother Wit Conference

The Mother Wit Conference started with a conversation between a mother & a daughter, and evolved into a dialogue between women of all ages and walks of life. Now it is a forum for men, women, families and communities to share, receive, transform and HEAL ♥

240 Years Ago - 1784

On December 5, 1784, at the age of 31, Phillis Wheatley dies in childbirth and so does her newborn daughter. Wheatly was an enslaved poet who became a literary celebrity in Boston. She was the first Black woman to become a notable poet in the United States.

220 Years Ago - 1804

Haiti gains independence on January 1, 1804. Victoria Montou, also known as “Gran Toya” and “Adbaraya Toya,” a skilled warrior, midwife, and healer who acted as a mentor and aunt-figure to Jean-Jacques Dessalines and served as a general of the revolutionary army was honored with the title of Imperial Duchess of the new nation.

170 Years Ago - 1854

On April 29, 1854, Ashmun Institute is established as the first college founded solely for Black American students. Renamed Lincoln University in 1866, it remains the nation's first degree-granting Historically Black College and University (HBCU).

160 Years Ago - 1864

Dr. Rebecca Lee Crumpler, a physician, nurse, and author, becomes the first Black woman in the US to earn an MD in 1864. Crumpler, an advocate of women and children’s health, championed the cause of medical training for nurses, and published a book on preventive health care.

160 Years Ago - 1864

Harriet Jacobs, abolitionist and author of Incidents In the Life of A Slave Girl (1861), opened the Jacobs School with her daughter on January 11, 1864. The school was open to formerly enslaved children and completely controlled by the black community.

160 Years Ago - 1864

Maggie Lena Walker, born on July 15, 1864, was an African American entrepreneur, civic leader, and teacher who became the first Black woman to establish and become president of a bank in the United States in 1903.

130 Years Ago - 1894

Bessie Smith is born on April 15, 1894. Smith was an African-American blues singer widely renowned during the Jazz Age. Nicknamed the "Empress of the Blues", she was the most popular female blues singer of the 1930s.

110 Years Ago - 1914

Marie Louise Bottineau Baldwin, attorney, accountant, and linguist, is the first Indigenous (Chippewa) law student to graduate from the Washington College of Law in 1914. She served as a Métis Turtle Mountain Band of Chippewa Indians attorney, Native American rights activist, and suffragist.

100 Years Ago - 1924

All Indigenous persons born in the U.S. are granted citizenship through the Indian Citizenship Act in 1924, and authorized to self-govern with limited government interventions through the Indian Reorganization Act of 1934.

100 Years Ago - 1924

The Harlem Renaissance began in 1924. This marked the beginning of an intellectual and cultural revival of African-American music, dance, art, fashion, literature, theater, politics and scholarship centered in Harlem, spanning the 1920s and 1930s.

100 Years Ago - 1924

Ella Jenkins, born August 6, 1924, is now 100! She is an American singer-songwriter and has been called "The First Lady of the Children's Folk Song." She has been a leading performer of folk and children's music.

100 Years Ago - 1924

Shirley Chisholm is born in Brooklyn, on November 30, 1924. Chisholm was an American politician who, in 1968, became the first black woman to be elected to the United States Congress and the first woman and African American to seek the nomination for president of the US.

100 Years Ago - 1924

Sonia Sanchez, a poet, activist, playwright, and teacher, is born on September 9, 1934. Sanchez was a leading figure in the Black Arts Movement and has written over 30 books including A Blues Book for Blue Black Magical Women (1974) and homegirls & handgrenades (1984).

90 Years Ago - 1934

Zora Neale Hurston publishes her first novel, Jonah's Gourd Vine (1934), a semi-autobiographical account of her parents' lives. This is the first of over 50 works written by Hurston, including Their Eyes Were Watching God (1937).

80 Years Ago - 1944

The United Negro College Fund is incorporated on April 25, 1944. The UNFC funds scholarships for black students and general scholarships for 37 private historically black colleges and universities (HBCUs).

80 Years Ago - 1944

Akasha Gloria Hull is born on December 6, 1944. Hull is a poet, educator, writer, critic, and a member of the Combahee River Collective. Her work in African-American literature and as a Black feminist activist has helped shape Women's Studies.

80 Years Ago - 1944

Angela Davis, a scholar, author, activist, and black feminist philosopher, is born on January 26, 1944. She is one of the most recognized political activists of the 1960s and 1970s and an advocate for prisoners' rights.

70 Years Ago - 1954

The Supreme Court decides Brown v. Board of Education on May 17, 1954, arguing that "separate but equal" schools are inherently unequal. The decision legally prohibits school segregation, declaring it unconstitutional.

60 Years Ago - 1964

In 1964, Constance Baker Motley became the first Black woman elected to the New York State Senate. As a key member of the NAACP Legal Defense and Educational Fund, Motley played a pivotal role in several significant cases, including Brown v. Board of Education (1954).

60 Years Ago - 1964

Twenty-fourth Amendment is ratified on January 23, 1964. This Amendment prohibits both Congress and the states to impose poll taxes or other types of tax for voting in federal elections.

60 Years Ago - 1964

The 1964 Philadelphia race riot, aka Columbia Avenue Riot, takes place in predominantly Black neighborhoods of North Philadelphia between August 28–30. Tensions between African American residents and police lead to 341 injuries and 774 arrests.

60 Years Ago - 1964

Congress passes the Civil Rights Act of 1964. This landmark legislation prohibited discrimination in public places, provided for the integration of schools and other public facilities, and made employment discrimination illegal.

60 Years Ago - 1964

Martin Luther King Jr. becomes the youngest recipient of the Nobel Peace Prize on October 14, 1964. After his death in 1968, Coretta Scott King, a lifelong civil rights activist continued to be active in the civil rights movement and as a social justice advocate.

50 Years Ago - 1974

National Congress of Neighborhood Women is formed in 1974 as a support group for grassroots women's organizations and community leaders involved in providing voices for poor and working-class women.

50 Years Ago - 1974

The Chicana Rights Project is created in 1974 as a feminist organization to address the legal rights of poor Mexican-American women. The project focused on addressing discrimination in employment, health, education, housing, and credit.

50 Years Ago - 1974

The Women's Educational Equity Act, signed into law on August 21, 1974, provides protections against gender discrimination in education, career counseling, sports education, and other programs. The law was introduced by Patsy Matsu Mink, the first woman of color and the first Asian-American woman elected to Congress.

50 Years Ago - 1974

The movie The Autobiography of Miss Jane Pittman starring Cicely Tyson premieres on January 31, 1974. Tyson, born in 1924, was known for her groundbreaking portrayals of strong, determined, elegant and dignified Black women.

50 Years Ago - 1974

Good Times debuts on February 8, 1974. The show was a television first for its portrayal of a two-parent African American family living in an inner-city housing project, showing the daily struggles and triumphs of the working-class.

40 Years Ago - 1984

The National Congress of Black Women founded in 1984, dedicated to the educational, political, economic and cultural development of African American women and their families.

40 Years Ago - 1984

Florence Griffith Joyner wins a silver medal in the 200-meter race at the 1984 Olympics in Los Angeles. Four years later (1988) she earned and continues to hold the world record as the fastest woman in the world.

40 Years Ago - 1984

bell hooks publishes Feminist Theory: From Margin to Center in 1984. The text highlights the ways the women's movement failed to address the needs of working women, especially those who were unfairly paid and treated unfairly in the workplace.

40 Years Ago - 1984

Octavia Butler publishes Clay's Ark in 1984. This is the last of Patternist series, a five novel collection she began working on in 1974, which explores themes of race, power dynamics, and the complexities of human nature.

30 Years Ago - 1994

The Violence Against Women Act of 1994 (VAWA) is signed into law on September 13, 1994. The VAWA creates and supports comprehensive, cost-effective responses to domestic violence, sexual assault, dating violence and stalking.

30 Years Ago - 1994

South Africa holds its first fully multiracial elections of April 27, 1994, marking the final end of apartheid. Nelson Mandela wins the elections and his wife Winnie Madikizela-Mandela, a longtime anti-apartheid activist, serves as a Member of Parliament for nearly 20 years.

30 Years Ago - 1994

In June of 1994, Toni M. Bond Leonard, Reverend Alma Crawford, Evelyn S. Field, Terri James, Bisola Marignay, Cassandra McConnell, Cynthia Newbille, Lorretta Ross, Elizabeth Terry, ‘Able’ Mable Thomas, Winnette P. Willis and Kim Youngblood coin the term reproductive justice.
They defined it as a “human right to maintain personal bodily autonomy, have children, not have children, and parent their children in safe and sustainable communities.”

20 Years Ago - 2004

The L Word starring Jennifer Beales premieres on January 18, 2004. The L Word featured television's first ensemble cast of lesbian, bisexual, and queer women. It was also the first television series written and directed by predominantly queer women.

10 Years Ago - 2014

The Affordable Care Act goes into effect on January 1, 2014, under the Obama administration, providing access to healthcare to more than 32.5 million uninsured or underinsured people.

10 Years Ago - 2014

Black-ish premieres on September 24th, 2014. The show addresses social issues, tackled emotional family problems such as death and divorce, and even got the audience to understand that growth is something that should happen within a family.
